You've seen it on a sports broadcast. You've heard it in the group chat. Someone says the Chiefs are -450 and everyone nods like they understand what that means.

Most don't. That's fine. Here's the actual explanation.

## The Sign Tells You Who's Expected to Win

A negative number means the sportsbook thinks that side is *likely* to win. A positive number means the sportsbook thinks that side is the *underdog*.

That's it. That's the first thing to internalize.

-270 means favorite. +220 means underdog.

## The Number Tells You the Price

Here's where it gets slightly more interesting.

**For negative odds (-270):** You would need to bet $270 to win $100 in profit.

**For positive odds (+220):** You would need to bet $100 to win $220 in profit.

Neither of those numbers is about $100 specifically - they're just the standard unit used for comparison. What matters is the *ratio*.

-270 means you're risking $2.70 for every $1 you stand to win.
+220 means you're risking $1 for every $2.20 you stand to win.

## Implied Probability: The Number Behind the Number

Every odds line has an implied probability baked into it. This is the number we care about most.

The formula for negative odds:

> **Implied probability = |odds| ÷ (|odds| + 100)**

So for -270: 270 ÷ (270 + 100) = 270 ÷ 370 = **72.97%**

The formula for positive odds:

> **Implied probability = 100 ÷ (odds + 100)**

So for +220: 100 ÷ (220 + 100) = 100 ÷ 320 = **31.25%**

## Why Implied Probabilities Don't Add Up to 100%

If you take any game and add up the implied probabilities for both sides, you'll get something like 104-106%. That's not a rounding error. That's the vig - the sportsbook's margin. It's how they make money regardless of the outcome.

The vig is why **odds and probability are related but not the same thing**. When we price scenarios at Odds Gods, we express the honest implied probability - no vig baked in, no house edge. Just the math.
