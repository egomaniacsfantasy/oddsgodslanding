Every sports argument is really a probability argument.

When someone says "the Chiefs are definitely winning the Super Bowl," they're not saying it's certain. They're saying they believe the probability is high enough that the alternative isn't worth serious consideration. When someone says "the Bills are finally going to break through," they're saying they believe the market is underpricing Buffalo's chances.

These are probability statements dressed up as opinions. The problem is that nobody has to be precise about them. You can say "definitely" and mean 60%. You can say "no chance" and mean 15%. The vagueness is the escape hatch.

We wanted to close the escape hatch.

## What Are the Odds?

The first tool we built is simple. You type any scenario in plain English - any NFL situation, any argument you've had at halftime, any take you've been carrying around for three weeks - and we give you one odds line. American format. Implied probability. The reasoning behind it.

Not a pick. Not a prediction. The honest price on the scenario as stated.

The goal is to make the argument more precise. Not to end it - the argument is the point - but to give everyone in the conversation the same number to push against.

## The Bracket Lab

The second tool does something we hadn't seen done cleanly before. You build a March Madness bracket. You click a team to lock in an outcome. Every other team's title odds reprice in real time based on that assumption.

The insight behind it is this: a bracket isn't a list of predictions. It's a set of conditional probabilities. Every pick is a dependency. Lock in a first-round upset and watch how it changes the implied odds for every team in that region. It's the difference between filling out a bracket and *understanding* the bracket.
