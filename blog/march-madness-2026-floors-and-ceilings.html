March Madness 2026: Initial Thoughts, Floors, and Ceilings

Franco Castagliuolo B.S. Data Analytics and Mathematical Sciences, Bentley University
March 16, 2026 12 min read

The bracket is set. Every team's season has been distilled down to a seeding, a region, and a path. What follows is a team-by-team breakdown of every program in the 2026 NCAA Tournament field, with realistic floors, upside ceilings, and the reasoning behind each projection.

Floors and ceilings are not predictions — they are the range of plausible outcomes given what we know about a team's strengths, vulnerabilities, and bracket position. A floor is the worst reasonable outcome, excluding catastrophic upsets. A ceiling is the best we can honestly expect, grounded in the numbers rather than hype. The model's 10,000-run Monte Carlo simulations inform these ranges but do not dictate them; basketball is not played on a spreadsheet.

A few themes define this field before we get into individual teams.

The East is loaded at the top. Duke and UConn are the two most complete programs in the country. Both have the talent and coaching to reach Atlanta. The path through the East is the hardest draw in the bracket.

Florida is the most complete team in the field. The Gators have the best efficiency differential, the deepest rotation, and the most favorable South draw. If this tournament goes chalky, Florida cuts the nets.

Michigan earned the Midwest 1 seed but the region has teeth. Iowa State and Kentucky are dangerous. This is not a relaxed path to the Final Four.

Purdue is underseeded. A 2 seed in the West feels like a gift. The Boilermakers are a legitimate national championship contender, and Arizona — the 1 seed in the same region — is the only program in the field we would argue is clearly better.

East Region

(1) Duke Blue Devils

FloorElite Eight CeilingNational Championship

Duke has been the best team in the ACC wire to wire, and the model agrees. Their offensive efficiency is elite — they space the floor, attack in transition, and limit turnovers at a rate few programs match. The ceiling is a national championship and it is not a stretch. The floor is Elite Eight because the East bracket will force them to beat at least one elite opponent before the Final Four, and on a bad night that game could end their run. But on a neutral floor with a week to prepare, Duke is one of the five programs in this field capable of winning every game they play.

(2) UConn Huskies

FloorElite Eight CeilingNational Championship

UConn is the defending dynasty of this era. Dan Hurley's program does not rebuild — it reloads. They defend at an elite level, play physically without fouling, and are almost impossible to beat in a half-court game. The concern is their half of the bracket may force a Duke matchup in the Elite Eight, which would be appointment television. Both teams are capable of winning that game. If UConn gets to the Final Four, they are a legitimate title threat no matter who they face.

(3) Michigan State Spartans

FloorSecond Round CeilingFinal Four

Tom Izzo teams do not get worse in March — they get better. Michigan State's schedule-adjusted numbers are strong enough to justify the 3 seed, and their experience in tournament settings is unmatched at this level. The floor is second round because their offense can be limited by physical, disciplined defenses, and they will see one in the Sweet Sixteen. But if Izzo gets them to Atlanta, nothing about that would be surprising.

(4) Kansas Jayhawks

FloorSecond Round CeilingElite Eight

Kansas is a program people are still treating like a dynasty, but this version of the Jayhawks is more vulnerable than their seeding implies. They defend reasonably well and have enough offensive weapons to advance past inferior opponents, but their efficiency numbers against top-40 defenses are concerning. The floor is second round because an upset-minded 5 or 13 seed could catch them. The ceiling is Elite Eight — they are not going to out-execute UConn or Duke in the bracket's top half.

(5) St. John's Red Storm

FloorSecond Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

St. John's winning the Big East Tournament was one of the best stories of conference tournament week. They play with an edge and their defense is genuinely disruptive. The model likes their profile more than the casual fan might — this is not a bubble team riding a hot week. Still, their path through the East is steep, and their offense relies too heavily on individual creation to sustain a deep run. Second round is the floor because they earned their bid. Sweet Sixteen is the ceiling unless their defense carries them to something special.

(6) Louisville Cardinals

FloorFirst Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Louisville is a physical team that can make life difficult for any opponent in a single game. They rebound, they defend in the paint, and they have players who can carry a game-winning performance on the right night. The concern is consistency — they have had too many flat offensive outings this season to feel safe in the first round. A first-round exit is a real possibility. If they make the second round, a Sweet Sixteen run against a fatigued or shorthanded opponent is not out of the question.

(7) UCLA Bruins

FloorFirst Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

UCLA has talent that does not always show up in results. They have underachieved relative to their roster this season, which cuts both ways in March — they could finally put it together, or the inconsistency could catch up to them in a one-game format. The model sees them as a genuine threat to the 2 seed if they play to their ceiling. If they come out flat, a first-round exit is on the table. There is real variance here.

(8) Ohio State Buckeyes

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Ohio State is in the field on the strength of a Big Ten schedule that does not gift easy wins. They grind, their defense is solid, and they protect the rim. But they are going to face Duke or UConn in the second round if they advance, which makes the Sweet Sixteen ceiling implausible. A first-round exit is possible — they match up poorly with several potential 9 seeds. If they win game one, they deserve credit and likely face a wall in game two.

(9) TCU Horned Frogs

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

TCU is one of the more interesting 9 seeds in the field. They have the athleticism and defensive energy to give Ohio State real problems. In a one-possession game, their length and activity can disrupt any opponent's offense. The ceiling is second round because their path gets exceedingly difficult after that — but pulling the first-round upset is genuinely plausible.

South Region

(1) Florida Gators

FloorElite Eight CeilingNational Championship

Florida is the model's top team in the field. Their adjusted efficiency differential is the best of any team in the tournament, they defend at an elite level, and they play through multiple capable scorers, making them difficult to game-plan against. The South bracket is manageable. Their floor is Elite Eight because no 1 seed exits earlier than that in our baseline projections, and Florida specifically has the depth to weather a cold shooting night. The ceiling is a national championship, and depending on how the bracket develops, they may enter the Final Four as the favorite regardless of opponent.

(2) Houston Cougars

FloorSweet Sixteen CeilingNational Championship

Houston under Kelvin Sampson is one of the sport's premier defensive programs. They are relentless, they guard without fouling, and they make possessions ugly for opponents who want to play fast. The concern entering this tournament is their offense — they have struggled to generate clean looks against top-20 defenses and can go through stretches where they cannot buy a basket. If they find an offensive rhythm, they can go all the way. The floor is Sweet Sixteen because a hot-shooting team can expose their offensive limitations in a single elimination game.

(3) Illinois Fighting Illini

FloorSecond Round CeilingFinal Four

Illinois has the size and physicality to make life difficult for anyone in this bracket. They protect the rim, rebound at an elite rate, and have guards who can create off the bounce and space the floor simultaneously. The floor is second round because they can be beaten by a hot-shooting team that takes away the paint. But the ceiling is genuine Final Four contention. If they get past the Sweet Sixteen, they will have beaten someone worth beating, and they will carry the model's respect going into Atlanta.

(4) Nebraska Cornhuskers

FloorSecond Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Nebraska has had a quietly impressive season. Their half-court offense is well-designed and they have enough rim protection to compete defensively in this field. The issue is that once they hit a top-20 opponent, their ability to keep pace offensively is questionable. Second round is the floor because they are legitimately good enough to win their first game. Sweet Sixteen is the ceiling because everything beyond that requires them to outperform their season-long efficiency against elite competition.

(5) Vanderbilt Commodores

FloorSecond Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Vanderbilt is here because they beat Florida twice and took down Arkansas in the SEC Tournament. That is not noise — that is a team that has proven it can beat elite competition. Their defense is underrated and their half-court execution is cleaner than most programs at this seed. The floor is second round and the ceiling is Sweet Sixteen, but do not be surprised if they push Houston in a potential second-round matchup. They have the defensive profile to make that game ugly.

(6) North Carolina Tar Heels

FloorFirst Round CeilingElite Eight

UNC is the program in this field with the widest range of outcomes. On their best day, they have the talent and tradition to reach the Elite Eight — they have done it repeatedly. On their worst day, they have shown a capacity for inexplicable losses to unranked opponents. The first-round floor is real because their defense has been porous at times and their effort level has been inconsistent. But if they arrive in March with something to prove, the ceiling is Elite Eight and it would not shock anyone.

(7) Saint Mary's Gaels

FloorFirst Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Saint Mary's is a Randy Bennett team, which means disciplined, efficient, and fundamentally sound in every aspect of the game. They do not beat themselves. They protect the ball, shoot threes selectively, and compete for 40 minutes. The ceiling is Sweet Sixteen because they can absolutely slow down a better team and steal two games. The floor is first round because their slower pace can be disrupted by a longer, more athletic team that pressures them into uncomfortable situations.

(8) Clemson Tigers

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Clemson is a hard-nosed defensive team that grinded through an ACC schedule including multiple top-15 opponents. They can make any game ugly and uncomfortable. The ceiling is second round because their half-court offense is limited enough that surviving into the Sweet Sixteen requires things to go very right. A first-round exit is a real possibility against a hot 9 seed.

(9) Iowa Hawkeyes

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Iowa plays through their post presence and has enough shooting to keep defenses honest. They are not a glamorous 9 seed, but they are a capable one. Against Clemson, they have the interior advantage. If they advance, they walk into a Florida or Houston buzz saw. First round to second round is the realistic window.

Midwest Region

(1) Michigan Wolverines

FloorSweet Sixteen CeilingNational Championship

Michigan earned the Midwest 1 seed through a Big Ten regular season that had no easy nights. They are long, athletic, and well-coached. Their offense has multiple creation modes and their defense communicates exceptionally well in transition. The floor is Sweet Sixteen rather than Elite Eight because Iowa State is capable of ending their run there, and the Midwest is not a comfortable path despite the 1 seed. The ceiling is a national championship — this team is complete enough to win it all, and the bracket gives them a path.

(2) Iowa State Cyclones

FloorSweet Sixteen CeilingFinal Four

Iowa State is a genuinely dangerous program in March. T.J. Otzelberger's defensive system is disruptive in ways that do not show up cleanly in box scores — they force opponents into uncomfortable shot selection and transition opportunities dry up fast. Their offense is not flashy but it is efficient and ball-movement oriented. The ceiling is Final Four because they can beat Michigan if the matchup goes a certain way. The floor is Sweet Sixteen because they are too good to lose in the first two rounds.

(3) Virginia Cavaliers

FloorSecond Round CeilingElite Eight

Virginia under Tony Bennett is perpetually on the list of teams that can reach the Elite Eight because they never beat themselves. Their pack-line defense makes games brutally slow and gives opponents fits when they rely on transition offense. The floor is second round because they will have at least one opponent who cannot figure out how to score on them. The ceiling is Elite Eight, and with their bracket position in the Midwest, a run to that point is a genuine possibility.

(4) Alabama Crimson Tide

FloorSecond Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Alabama is an athletic, physically imposing team that can overwhelm lesser opponents in the open floor. Their pace and length create problems for half-court defenses. The issue is that when teams slow them down and force them into structured half-court possessions, their shot selection becomes erratic. Second round is the floor because they are talented enough to advance. Sweet Sixteen is the ceiling — their defensive inconsistency limits their upside against precision offenses.

(5) Texas Tech Red Raiders

FloorSecond Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Texas Tech remains one of the sport's premier defensive programs. They are physical, disciplined, and capable of making any offense look broken for 35 minutes. The ceiling is Sweet Sixteen because if their three-point shooting gets hot, they have the defensive backbone to hold a lead. The floor is second round — their half-court defense is too reliable to fail in the first game.

(6) Tennessee Volunteers

FloorSecond Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Tennessee is another program built on defensive physicality and rebounding dominance. Rick Barnes has the Vols playing their best basketball in late February and March. Their concern is a potential mismatch against a quicker, more skilled offensive team that can get into the lane at will. Second round floor, Sweet Sixteen ceiling — though they have the defensive profile to push it further on a good night.

(7) Kentucky Wildcats

FloorSecond Round CeilingElite Eight

Kentucky has the talent to go anywhere in this bracket. Mark Pope has rebuilt this program faster than most people expected, and the Wildcats have NBA-caliber talent at multiple positions. Their ceiling is Elite Eight and it is not a reach — if they are playing their best basketball, they can beat Iowa State. The floor is second round because there are games where their young core plays like freshmen, and one of those games in March ends their tournament early.

(8) Georgia Bulldogs

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Georgia is the most dangerous 8 seed in the bracket for Kentucky. They play with energy and have enough perimeter shooting to compete in any single game. The ceiling is second round — if they upset Kentucky, they walk into Michigan or Iowa State, which is not survivable. A first-round exit is also plausible. This is a one-game team with a clear ceiling.

(9) Saint Louis Billikens

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Saint Louis is a disciplined program with good ball movement and a defensive identity. They made the tournament on merit and can compete in a first-round game. The ceiling is second round — if they pull the upset, they have earned respect. First-round exit is more likely given the strength of their potential opponent.

(10) Santa Clara Broncos

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Santa Clara is here because they can shoot threes from anywhere and they play fearlessly in big games. Their conference tournament run was convincing. The ceiling is second round and they are the type of team that bracket players will be picking as a sleeper. The floor is first round — they will see a more athletic opponent who can defend the perimeter and control the tempo.

(12) Akron Zips

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Akron won the MAC Tournament and earned their spot. They are a physical team with a legitimate scorer who can carry them on the right night. The classic 12-5 upset is always in play in March, and Akron has the profile — physical, disciplined, with a capable primary option. A first-round exit is the most likely outcome, but the second round ceiling is real.

West Region

(1) Arizona Wildcats

FloorSweet Sixteen CeilingNational Championship

Arizona is the model's second-ranked team in the field and a legitimate national championship contender. Tommy Lloyd's system produces elite offensive efficiency — they share the ball, they space the floor, and they have multiple players who can create in isolation or out of the pick-and-roll. The floor is Sweet Sixteen rather than Elite Eight because their defense has been inconsistent this season, and a hot-shooting team can expose them in a single game. The ceiling is a national championship. If Arizona is playing their best basketball in Atlanta, they are a nightmare to stop.

(2) Purdue Boilermakers

FloorElite Eight CeilingNational Championship (Final)

Purdue feels underseeded at 2. Their interior dominance is unprecedented in this year's field — no team in the country has a more reliable post presence, and they complement it with capable perimeter shooting and experience. Matt Painter has this group locked in. The floor is Elite Eight because they have the size and experience to absorb almost anything a first or second weekend opponent throws at them. The ceiling is a national championship. If Purdue meets Arizona in the West final, that is one of the tournament's best potential matchups — post dominance versus ball movement and perimeter creation. Whoever wins that game is a Final Four darling.

(3) Gonzaga Bulldogs

FloorSweet Sixteen CeilingFinal Four

Gonzaga is Gonzaga. Mark Few's program finds a way to make deep tournament runs because they play the right way — fast, efficient, and with an offensive system that punishes teams that go under screens. The concern is that their schedule this year was lighter than it has been historically, and their efficiency numbers against top-25 defenses are not as clean as their overall numbers suggest. The floor is Sweet Sixteen because they will advance past the first weekend. The ceiling is Final Four, because beating Arizona or Purdue requires them to play their best basketball of the year.

(4) Arkansas Razorbacks

FloorSecond Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Arkansas is an SEC team built on athleticism, pressure defense, and fast-break opportunities. They can run teams off the floor on the right night. The concern is that in slow, half-court tournament environments, their offense can stagnate. Second round is the floor — they are too talented to lose in the first round. Sweet Sixteen is the ceiling because Gonzaga or the team coming out of the 2-7 matchup presents a very difficult half-court challenge.

(5) Wisconsin Badgers

FloorFirst Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Wisconsin is Wisconsin — they run the offense slowly, protect the ball, and make opponents earn everything. Their efficiency in the half court is consistently elite. The floor is first round because a long, athletic team can disrupt their slower pace and create problems in transition. The ceiling is Sweet Sixteen, and it is attainable. Wisconsin beats teams they are not supposed to beat in March because they are patient and disciplined, two qualities that are rare in the tournament environment.

(6) BYU Cougars

FloorFirst Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

BYU has the perimeter shooting to beat anyone in the field on a hot night. Their offense is modern and well-spaced. The concern is their defense has been exploitable this season by teams with capable interior scorers. They would face Purdue's post presence in the Sweet Sixteen, which is a difficult stylistic matchup. Floor is first round, ceiling is Sweet Sixteen, and they need to find a way to slow down elite bigs to get there.

(7) Miami (FL) Hurricanes

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Miami is an experienced team with capable guards and a defensive edge that has won them games this season. They can knock off Villanova if they play with their characteristic intensity. A first-round exit is also possible against a Villanova program that has been doing this for decades. The ceiling is second round — advancing past that requires things to go very right against a higher-caliber opponent.

(8) Villanova Wildcats

FloorFirst Round CeilingSweet Sixteen

Villanova is a program. They recruit for the tournament, they prepare for the tournament, and they consistently deliver performances that exceed their seed. The ceiling is Sweet Sixteen — this program, even in a post-dynasty cycle, knows how to win in March. The floor is first round because Miami has the personnel to disrupt their system. A potential 8-1 matchup against Arizona in the second round would be one of the tournament's best games.

(9) Utah State Aggies

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

Utah State won the Mountain West Tournament and is here playing confident basketball. They can shoot and they compete. Against Gonzaga, they are significant underdogs, but they have the perimeter skills to make it uncomfortable. A first-round exit is more likely, but the second-round ceiling exists if Gonzaga is not fully locked in.

(12) High Point Panthers

FloorFirst Round CeilingSecond Round

High Point is a Big South automatic qualifier who plays with pace and has a genuine go-to scorer. They are not here by accident and they will compete. Against Wisconsin's patient, physical system, they face a tough stylistic matchup. The classic 12-5 upset possibility is diminished here by Wisconsin's discipline, but tournament basketball has taught us to never say never.

Final Thoughts

The model's top four championship contenders heading into the tournament are Florida, Arizona, Duke, and Purdue — in that order. These are the four programs with the combination of elite efficiency, defensive reliability, and bracket positioning to reach and win in Atlanta.

The upset watches that most interest us: Tennessee over Alabama in the Midwest second round, Virginia making the Elite Eight, St. John's advancing out of the second round in the East, and Utah State giving Gonzaga a scare in the first round.

The team the model likes least given their seeding: Kansas as a 4 seed. Their numbers against top-40 defenses are not convincing, and a first-round exit is not out of the question.

Play your bracket. Trust the numbers when it is comfortable, and go with your gut when it is not. That is the game, and it is why we love it.
